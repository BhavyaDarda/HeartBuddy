General Information

Demographia

>> Age:
The risk of heart disease increases with age. Men over the age of 45 and women over the age of 55 are at higher risk of developing heart disease.
>> Gender:
Men are more likely to develop heart disease than premenopausal women. However, after menopause, women's risk of heart disease increases, and it becomes similar to that of men.
>> Race/ethnicity:
Some racial and ethnic groups have a higher risk of heart disease than others. For example, African Americans, Hispanic/Latino Americans, and Native Americans have a higher risk of heart disease than non-Hispanic whites.
>> Geographic location:
People living in certain regions may have a higher risk of heart disease due to environmental factors such as air pollution, lack of access to healthy food, and limited opportunities for physical activity.

Lifestyle

>> Poor Diet:
A diet high in saturated and trans fats, cholesterol, salt, and added sugars can increase the risk of heart disease. Eating a diet high in fruits, vegetables, whole grains, lean protein, and healthy fats can help reduce the risk of heart disease.
>> Lack of Physical Activity:
Physical inactivity can increase the risk of heart disease. Regular physical activity, such as brisk walking, cycling, swimming, or dancing, can help reduce the risk of heart disease.
>> Smoking:
Smoking is a major risk factor for heart disease. Quitting smoking can significantly reduce the risk of heart disease.
>> Excessive Alcohol Consumption:
Excessive alcohol consumption can increase the risk of high blood pressure, heart failure, and stroke. Limiting alcohol consumption to moderate levels can help reduce the risk of heart disease.
>> Obesity and Overweight:
Being overweight or obese can increase the risk of heart disease. Maintaining a healthy weight through a balanced diet and regular physical activity can help reduce the risk of heart disease.
>> Stress:
Chronic stress can contribute to the development of heart disease. Stress management techniques such as meditation, deep breathing, and yoga can help reduce stress and the risk of heart disease.

Medical

>> High Blood Pressure (Hypertension):
High blood pressure can increase the workload on the heart and blood vessels, leading to the development of heart disease.
>> High Cholesterol:
High levels of cholesterol in the blood can contribute to the development of plaque in the arteries, increasing the risk of heart disease.
>> Diabetes:
People with diabetes are at higher risk of developing heart disease due to the impact of high blood sugar levels on blood vessels and the heart.
>> Family History of Heart Disease:
Having a close family member, such as a parent or sibling, with heart disease increases an individual's risk of developing heart disease.

About Us

Heart Disease Prediction by Bhavya Darda, Shuchi Sneh, Saanvi, Samridhi Singh

Heart disease remains a significant global health concern, and despite advancements in medical science and healthcare practices, there is an ongoing need for more accurate, timely, and personalized methods of risk assessment and prediction. The existing healthcare system primarily relies on traditional risk assessment tools, which can be limited in their ability to capture the full range of risk factors. Moreover, these tools often lack the granularity and predictive power to identify individuals at the earliest stages of heart disease development or those at higher risk within specific subpopulations.
